Seattle, drawn in drive time

A map where distance means time. Two places are drawn close together if driving between them is quick, and far apart if it's slow, no matter how near they are on a normal map.

How to read it

Drag the slider. At the left, every dot sits at its true location: it's an ordinary map of Seattle. As you drag right, each dot moves to where it belongs in time. Watch three things happen:

The city gets wider. East-west trips are slow, so east-west distances grow. North-south trips ride I-5 and Highway 99, so those distances barely change. Seattle's whole shape stretches sideways.

The red lines stretch the most. Those are the bridges. Nearly every trip across the Ship Canal or the Duwamish funnels over a handful of crossings, and the waiting is baked into every trip that uses them. In time-space, the two banks of the canal are simply farther apart than they look, and the bridges visibly lengthen to span the gap.

Some dots are bigger than others. A flat map can't honor every travel time at once, so each dot's size shows how much the map is "lying" right there. The big dots cluster near the water crossings, where two places can be close in a straight line and far apart by car.

Why Seattle?

Every city has traffic, but Seattle's is unusually directional. The city is a north-south strip pinched between Puget Sound and Lake Washington, with a canal cut across its middle. Fast roads run the long way; the short way crosses water. That means how long a trip takes depends heavily on which direction you're going, which is exactly the thing a normal map can't show and this one can.

What's actually going on

A grid of about 500 points covers the city. For every pair of points we have a driving time, and the computer looks for an arrangement of the points where the distances on screen match those times as closely as possible. No one draws the stretched map by hand; it's what falls out when you ask "what would Seattle look like if distance meant time?" and let the times answer.

If you want the details, including why a perfect time-map is mathematically impossible and what we do about it, the math page walks through it with pictures.
