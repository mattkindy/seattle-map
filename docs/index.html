An Interactive Map

Seattle, if Distance Were Measured in Minutes

Redraw the city so that places sit as far apart as they feel from the driver's seat, and Seattle changes shape: the bridges stretch, and the far side of the water gets farther.

Two ways to measure the drive: posted speed limits on an open road, and live traffic captured on a Friday evening. See the note below.

Ballard and Magnolia face each other across Salmon Bay, close enough that a walker can cross between them at the locks in a few minutes. A driver cannot. The car trip loops inland to the Ballard Bridge and back around, and on a bad afternoon it can take twenty minutes. Ask a Seattle driver and they will tell you the two neighborhoods are nowhere near each other.

The map below redraws the city with driving time as its measure of distance. Drag the slider and watch Seattle move from the map you know to the shape of its drives. The buttons switch between an empty-roads city and the same city at Friday rush.

Five drives

Ballard and the University District are three miles apart. On open roads the drive takes about nine minutes; on a Friday evening, eleven. Because the trip has to cross the Ship Canal, this map draws the two neighborhoods 38 percent farther apart than geography would. Each row below reads the same way, and tapping one traces its route on the map.

Minutes are the average of the two directions. Free flow drives every road at its posted limit; Friday evening slows the same roads by measured traffic.

What to look for

North-south distances compress as you drag. Interstate 5 and Highway 99 make north-south driving fast, so those trips take fewer minutes than their mileage suggests, and the city draws taller and narrower.

The bridges stretch. A trip across the Ship Canal or the Duwamish has to reach a crossing and back, so the two banks sit a little farther apart in minutes than in miles, and the red bridge lines lengthen to match.

Then switch the map to Friday evening. Every trip gets longer, the average by about a fifth, but not evenly. Congestion cuts the freeway advantage, so north-south trips lose part of their compression, while the crossings and the routes into downtown stretch further. The two maps are the same city measured at two different hours.

Some dots grow as you drag. A flat map cannot match every driving time at once, so some drawn distances end up off. A dot's size shows how far off the distances around it ended up: the map's error bars, drawn in place.

Why Seattle

Seattle's roads are unusually directional. The city is a north-south strip between Puget Sound and Lake Washington, and its only fast roads, Interstate 5 and Highway 99, run that direction. East-west trips have no highway and move on surface streets. On top of that, the Ship Canal cuts the city in half, so trips between the north and south halves funnel across a handful of bridges. Travel time here depends on direction more than in most American cities, and that difference is what an ordinary map cannot show.

A computer routed roughly 270,000 point-to-point drives for each version of the map over the street network and arranged about 700 points so that the distances between them match the times as closely as possible. The result is what Seattle looks like when distance means time. How we made this map walks through the method, with pictures.
