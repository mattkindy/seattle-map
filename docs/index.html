An Interactive Map

Seattle, if Distance Were Measured in Minutes

Redraw the city so that places sit as far apart as they feel from the driver's seat, and Seattle changes shape: the bridges stretch, and the far side of the water gets farther.

The times behind this version are modeled, not measured. See the note on the data below.

Ballard and the University District are about three miles apart, a 12-minute errand on paper. But the Ship Canal lies between them, and nearly every car that makes the trip must funnel across one of a handful of bridges. Ask a Seattle driver and they will tell you the two neighborhoods are not really three miles apart at all.

The map below takes the driver's side of the argument. It places every point in the city at a distance that reflects driving time rather than mileage. Drag the slider and watch the city move from the map you know to the city you actually live in.

the map the drive

Each dot is a sample point. Red lines are the bridges. Larger dots mark places where no flat map can fully honor the driving times.

What to look for

The city grows wider as you drag. North-south trips ride Interstate 5 and Highway 99, so those distances barely move. East-west trips crawl across the grid, so the map stretches sideways to make room for the lost minutes.

The bridges stretch most of all. In clock terms, the two banks of the Ship Canal are simply farther apart than they look, and the red crossings lengthen to span the difference. The same happens over the Duwamish on the way to West Seattle.

And some dots swell. A flat map cannot satisfy every driving time at once; somewhere, it has to fudge. The dot size shows how much fudging each place required. The largest dots cluster at the water crossings, which is Seattle's traffic story told in a single statistic.

Why Seattle bends this way

Most cities have traffic. Seattle has geometry. The city is a north-south strip pinched between Puget Sound and Lake Washington and cut across its waist by a canal. The fast roads run the long way; the short way crosses water. Travel time here depends on compass direction to a degree few American cities match, and that directional difference is precisely what an ordinary map cannot show.

No cartographer drew the stretched city. A computer was given roughly 130,000 point-to-point driving times and asked to arrange 500 points so that the distances between them match the clock as closely as possible. The shape that emerges is the answer to a question: what would Seattle look like if distance meant time? How we made this map walks through the method, with pictures.

About the data. The driving times in this version come from a simplified traffic model of Seattle (fast north-south corridors, slower surface streets, delays at the water crossings), built so the method could be developed and judged before collecting real data. Measured drive times from a routing service are the planned replacement and slot into the same pipeline without changing the map's machinery. Code and pipeline: github.com/mattkindy/seattle-map.
