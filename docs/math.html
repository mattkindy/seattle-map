Methodology

How We Made This Map

Turning 130,000 driving times into one stretched picture of Seattle takes five steps. None requires more than high-school math to follow. Back to the map.

Step 1: Sample the city

Measuring the travel time from everywhere to everywhere is impossible, so the city is reduced to a honeycomb of roughly 500 sample points, one every 600 meters or so, with the ones that fall in water discarded. Everything that follows concerns these points; the rest of the city moves with its nearest neighbors.

Step 2: Time every pair

Each pair of points gets a driving time, from a routing service or, in the current version, from a simplified model of Seattle traffic. With 500 points that is about 130,000 numbers, kept in a square table: row for the start, column for the destination. One wrinkle: the drive from A to B rarely takes exactly as long as the drive back, and a flat drawing can show only one distance per pair, so the two directions are averaged.

Step 3: Let springs draw the city

Here is the heart of the method. Imagine every pair of points joined by a spring whose natural length is their driving time. Short trips pull points together; long trips hold them apart. Release the tangle and let it settle. The arrangement it relaxes into is the time-map: the layout in which distances on the page match times on the clock as closely as they can.

The formal name is multidimensional scaling. A piece of linear algebra known as classical MDS produces a good starting arrangement in a single computation, and an iterative refinement called SMACOF then plays out the settling: each point repeatedly asks, for every other point, where it would need to stand for their distance to come out exactly right, and moves to the average of the answers. Each round provably reduces the total mismatch.

Step 4: Turn it right side up

The spring layout is right about distances and silent about direction. It emerges arbitrarily rotated, and sometimes mirrored, because turning or flipping the whole picture changes no distance within it. A final step slides, turns and, if needed, un-mirrors the layout until it agrees with real geography as closely as possible, a technique called Procrustes alignment. That is why north stays up and the result still reads as Seattle. An early version of this site applied the rotation with its sign flipped, and the whole city visibly spun as the slider moved; the error is preserved here as a caution about checking alignment numerically.

Step 5: Measure what the map cannot say

Now the deep limitation, which is also the most interesting part. Take two spots facing each other across the Ship Canal: 15 minutes apart by car, and each 10 minutes from the bridge that connects them. Try to draw all three so that every distance matches its time. It cannot be done. The two banks want to sit far apart, yet both must stay close to the same bridge, which drags them back together. Travel time simply does not behave like distance on a flat sheet of paper.

The layout is therefore a best compromise, and the compromise is measured. The leftover error at each point is called stress, and it is what the dot sizes on the map encode. High stress marks the places where geography and the clock disagree most sharply. In Seattle those places are the water crossings, which is the story of the city told in one statistic.

A note on the data

The current site runs on a modeled time table: fast north-south corridors, slower surface streets, penalties at the crossings. The model exists so the method could be built and judged before collecting real measurements. Swapping in measured times from a routing service changes only Step 2; every other step is untouched. The full pipeline is about 500 lines of dependency-free JavaScript, published in the repository.
