The math behind the time-map

How 130,000 driving times become one stretched picture of Seattle, in five steps. Each has a picture; none needs more than high-school math to follow. Back to the map.

1. Sample the city

You can't measure the travel time from everywhere to everywhere, so we lay a honeycomb of about 500 points over the city, roughly one every 600 meters, and skip the ones that land in water. Everything from here on is about these points; the rest of the city tags along with its nearest points.

2. Measure every pair

For each pair of points we get a driving time, either from a routing service or (in the current version) from a stand-in model of Seattle traffic. With 500 points that's about 130,000 numbers, which we keep in a big square table: row = start, column = destination. One detail: A→B and B→A can differ a little, and a flat drawing can only show one distance per pair, so we average the two directions.

3. Let springs find the layout

Now the key move. Imagine every pair of points connected by a spring whose natural length is their driving time. Short times want to pull points together; long times push them apart. Release the whole tangle and let it settle. Where it comes to rest is the time-map: the arrangement where screen distances match driving times as well as they possibly can.

The algorithm is called multidimensional scaling. A clever bit of linear algebra (worth looking up: classical MDS) finds a good starting arrangement in one shot, and then an iterative step (SMACOF) plays out the spring-settling: each point repeatedly asks, for every other point, "where would I need to stand for our distance to be exactly right?" and moves to the average of the answers. Every round provably lowers the total mismatch.

4. Turn it right side up

The spring layout is correct about distances but says nothing about direction: it comes out arbitrarily rotated, and sometimes mirrored, because turning or flipping the whole picture changes no distance. So the final step slides, turns, and if needed un-mirrors the layout until it lines up with real geography as closely as possible (the technique is called Procrustes alignment). That's why north is up and the map still reads as Seattle. We got this wrong at first, with a sign error applying the rotation backwards, and the map visibly spun as it morphed.

5. What a flat map can't do

Here's the deep limitation, and the most interesting part. Take two spots facing each other across the Ship Canal: 15 minutes apart by car (over a bridge), and each 10 minutes from that bridge. Try to draw all three with distances matching times. You can't. The two banks want to be far apart, but they're both close to the same bridge, which drags them back together. Travel time just doesn't behave like distance on a flat sheet.

So the layout is a best compromise, and we measure how much each point had to compromise. That number is called stress, and it's what the dot sizes on the map show. High stress isn't failure; it's the map pointing at the places where geography and time disagree the most. In Seattle, that's the water crossings, which is the story of the city in one statistic.

Fine print

The current site runs on a modeled time table (fast north-south, slow east-west, penalties at the crossings), which is enough to demonstrate the machinery. Swapping in measured times from a routing API changes only step 2; everything else is untouched. The pipeline is about 500 lines of dependency-free JavaScript, and the code for every step is in the repository.
